Fish for Cheap

Issa Fall looks exhausted. «Didn’t catch much today either, just about enough to pay for the fuel». A fisherman since birth and going back «five generations», he heads to sea every morning at 9 and returns in the afternoon to sell his catch at the makeshift market on the shore at Soumbedioune, on the outskirts of Dakar. This sleepy little bay rattles to life each day after 4pm, with men unloading crates, women yelling out prices among improvised stalls, and dozens of customers that select, muse over, haggle for and eventually buy the goods amid all the clamour. But the atmosphere is anything but festive: one look at the group of fishermen as they park their canoes is enough to take in the full scale of the crisis that has gripped the whole sector for some time now. The baskets are half empty, the faces are long. The bounty of the past is long gone: the sea not as generous as it used to be, «when you could almost fish with your hands».



















Fall, who acts as a spokesman for his community, has no doubts whatsoever: the fault lies squarely with the foreign trawlers that come to fish in Senegalese waters, with the tacit approval of the central government. «First it was the Russians and the Asians that came to plunder us. Now they’ve reopened the sea for the Europeans».
He is furious about the new agreement that Dakar has signed with the European Union. Sealed in November 2014, it grants 36 licenses to 8 pole-and-liners and 28 fishing boats with surrounding nets (purse seiners) that primarily catch tuna. It also opens up the sea to two large trawlers for deep-sea fishing, on the hunt for black codfish. In return, Senegal will receive «financial compensation» totalling 13.9 million euros for the five years that the agreement covers, to be provided by the European Union (63%) and the ship owners (37%).


The result of many months of negotiations that many define as far from transparent, this agreement is now at the centre of debate in a country that sees 17% of its active population working in the fishing sector. The owners of the European boats counter the criticism by the local fishermen with the claim that in contrast with what has happened in the past, today there is no overlapping competition between the two kinds of fishing. «This agreement is not a negative development. It mostly concerns tuna, which is practically never caught by canoe fishermen», argues David Rigat, General Manager of CMNP, which protects the interests of a number of European ship owners in the port of Dakar.
Suspended in 2006 precisely because of exploitation of fishing resources, the agreement has been revived in another form: whereas previously access was granted to all species, the present agreement mostly pertains to tuna and forms part of a series of bilateral accords that allow European fishing vessels to follow the tropical tuna along its migratory path in the Gulf of Guinea, from Angola to Mauritania.

The contested ocean















At Dakar port’s Quay number 10, at the offices of Sopasen, the mixed French-Senegalese administration company, Adama Lam is livid: «the government has bowed to foreign interests». Vice-president of the Ship owners and Industrial Fishing Processors Association of Senegal (GAIPES), Lam makes no bones about calling this «neo-colonialism». «We asked for the granting of tuna fishing licenses and were refused, on the pretext that the stock was overexploited. Straight after, we saw the government sign an agreement with the Europeans. It’s an absolute scandal». Sopasen, like the other mixed companies that operate at Dakar port, fish mostly for exportation: shrimp, oily fish, octopus and other species destined for the European market, but also for Asia and for African countries with greater buying power, such as the Ivory Coast. «But it’s tuna that allows us industrial processors to make more profit», stresses Lam.

















The Governing Board for Maritime Resources has no time for such accusations. «The fishing agreement concerns tuna», their Senior Manager, Mamadou Goudiaby, is at pains to repeat. «Given that Senegal has the potential to fish approximately 14,000 tons and our ship owners are not capable of fishing such an amount, we independently decided to sign an agreement with Brussels». The UE-Senegal agreement does indeed mention 14,000 tons, but this is merely a reference figure. Were this amount to be exceeded, the agreement foresees the payment of between 50 and 70 euros per each additional ton. «It’s a ridiculous amount», Lam points out. «Such an amount can be fished by the purse seiners in three weeks. The truth is that the European ship owners have been given carte blanche to loot our sea to their hearts content».
A minimum of number crunching would seem to justify his claims: If you sum the amount fished by the 8 European pole-and-liners that have been active in recent years as a result of private agreements with the government and those fished by the 6 Senegalese pole-and-liners, you already reach the 14,000 ton threshold. The arrival of another 28 purse-seiners will inevitably lead to a crossing of this threshold. «We’re selling off a precious resource for a handful of peanuts», thunders Fatou Niang, Manager of Senevisa, one of the main mixed capital Senegalese fishing companies in operation at Dakar port.



Amount of tuna caught outside the agreement

During the years when the agreement EU-Senegal had been discontinued, the few pole-and-liners operating in Senegal were already fishing the amount of tuna indicated in the new agreement. The arrival of another 28 big seiners will inevitably lead to a crossing of this threshold, denounce both the industrialist and the environmentalists .

Fishermen without nets















It is not only the industrialists and fishermen that are raising the barricades against the agreement. Environmental organisations are also voicing deep concern. «Fishing with surrounding nets is not selective; it will inevitably entail the capture of other kinds of fish and cause an impoverishment of the ecosystem», worries Ahmed Diami, Director of the Oceans campaign for Greenpeace Africa, «not to mention the fact that the species of tuna that are being targeted are at risk of overexploitation».














The control of tuna stock is overseen by the ICCAT (International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tunas), which has to make the necessary evaluations and determine whether or not it is necessary to impose quotas or even a complete ban on fishing, as occurred for example in the case of red tuna in the Mediterranean. But for the main species of tropical tuna caught off the coast of West Africa – the yellow fin and the skipjack – so far the ICCAT has not imposed any limit on fishing. So the Europeans can fish at will. «And who is to say that the government won’t sign agreements with other countries?», Diami asks himself. «Is it going to say no to the Russians or the Chinese if they come looking for similar licenses?». When this is put to the Manager of the Fisheries Department, the answer given would do little to reassure the Greenpeace Director of Campaign: «We do not exclude this. We will examine each case in its own right. Our obligation is to make the most of our natural resources», says Goudiaby.


It is precisely this question of making the most of resources that has led to the most heated arguments. Many consider the financial compensation to be too low and the return for the local sector to be minimal. Greenpeace asks why there was such haste to sign the agreement while a new code on fishing was under discussion, a code that was to take into consideration the difficulties of local fishermen. And why did it also include the possibility of fishing not only tuna but also black codfish, with the use of two trawlers. «The inclusion of these two fishing vessels in the agreement totally contradicts the mandate of local fishing policy, by which agreements should only be made in the case of a surplus and a clear inability on the part of local fishermen to capture the resource», underlines Béatrice Gorez, who coordinates the «Coalition for Fair Fisheries Arrangements» in Brussels.

The sea in a can















But where does all this tuna fished in the waters of West Africa end up? Most of it is loaded onto freezer ships destined for Spain’s Galicia, the world’s main region for canned tuna production (along with Thailand). Each day the ports of Ribeira and Puebla see the unloading of tons of tuna that are then transferred to various factories in the region belonging to groups such as Jealsa-Rianxeira, Calvo or Garavilla, which then distribute the canned produce throughout the European market.  Ever since 1953, when some Basque fishermen ventured out in wooden boats to the waters off the African coast, the Spanish have been capturing tropical tuna. Their practical know-how has helped them build a well-structured industry, which has been strengthened over the years by virtue of European fishery agreements. In his Madrid office, Julio Morón, President of OPAGAC, an umbrella group for a number of purse-seiner owners involved in the agreement, traces the history of the African fishing campaigns by Spanish fishermen and the successive agreements that have materialised. As far as he is concerned, the criticism by the Senegalese industrialists is highly spurious. «Tuna is a migratory species; it doesn’t live in Senegalese waters. It is only passing through. They could fish it just like us, but in all this time without a clear agreement they haven’t been able to develop a fishing industry».












Whether they haven’t managed because of a lack of support by the state, because of foreign competition or simply because their entrepreneurial ability was found wanting is difficult to say. One thing is certain, however: the presence of a European fleet does nothing to help their development. Most of the fleet consists of ships belonging to large groups who have obtained various kinds of subsidies over the years, such as for shipbuilding or for fuel. Although this subsidy system has been changed by the new Common Fisheries Policy, which brings a considerable reduction of subsidies and a limitation of the worst abuses, there is no doubt that the European ship owners have enjoyed significant assistance over the years. Even today, the payment by Brussels for the licenses to fish in Senegalese waters is a public subsidy, which is being handed over to private interests. «The taxes of European citizens are funding the plunder of African resources by big business», is Adama Lam’s summary of the situation.



The small fishermen observe this clash between the Senegalese industrialists and the Europeans from the shoreline where their canoes are parked. It is true that tuna is not one of the main species that they catch. But what they ask themselves is this: What benefits are we going to see from this? What are we going to earn from this agreement? According to the contract, a part of the funds provided by the European Union should serve to «promote the sustainable development of local fishing through sectorial support». There’s been no sign of this so far.
Issa Fall believes that all he can count on is his own endeavour, and goes to sea each morning along with the other approximately 21,600 canoes registered in Senegal. Whenever they see the foreign ships in the distance, he and all the other fishermen can’t help but ask themselves why the Europeans have to come to fish right here, and why, as each day goes by, the number of fish in their own nets gets smaller.
